Supplier Verification

How to Verify a Chinese Supplier Before You Pay

A practical step-by-step guide to checking a Chinese supplier’s legal identity, bank details, production capability, quality system, and commercial reliability.

Published 2026-07-1311 min readBy Ensamico

Finding a supplier is easy. Confirming that the company is legally real, commercially capable, and suitable for your order is the harder part.

A proper verification process should answer five separate questions:

  • Does the legal company exist and remain in normal operating status?
  • Does the person you are speaking with genuinely represent that company?
  • Does the payment account belong to the correct legal entity?
  • Can the supplier actually manufacture or reliably control the product?
  • Are the supplier’s commercial terms, quality systems, and communication strong enough for your risk level?

Registration checks are necessary, but they are only the first layer. A company can be legally registered and still be a poor supplier.

1. Ask for the supplier’s full legal identity

Do not begin with a brand name, English nickname, or Alibaba store name. Ask for:

  • Full Chinese legal company name
  • Unified Social Credit Code
  • Business licence copy
  • Registered address
  • Legal representative’s name
  • Chinese company bank beneficiary name
Important: The company name on the contract, invoice, business licence, and payment instructions should be consistent. Any difference should be explained in writing before payment.

2. Check the official enterprise record

Use China’s official market-regulation systems to review the company record. Search by the Chinese legal name or Unified Social Credit Code. Check:

  • Registration status
  • Date of establishment
  • Registered capital
  • Business scope
  • Legal representative
  • Registered address
  • Shareholders and annual-report information where available
  • Abnormal-operation or administrative-penalty records

Pay special attention to whether the business scope is reasonably connected to the product or service being offered. A broad business scope is common in China, so this is a clue rather than final proof.

3. Verify the sales contact’s authority

A genuine company can still be impersonated. Confirm that your contact is connected to the company by using more than one channel:

  • Send a confirmation email to the supplier’s company domain
  • Call the company’s publicly listed number
  • Ask for a company-stamped quotation or contract
  • Confirm the contact’s name and role with another employee
  • Use a video call from the office or factory when practical

Free email addresses are not automatically fraudulent, but they provide less assurance than a functioning company domain.

4. Match payment details to the transaction

Before transferring funds, compare the beneficiary name against the contract and business licence. Be cautious when:

  • The beneficiary is a private individual
  • The account belongs to an unrelated third party
  • The supplier suddenly changes bank details
  • The invoice is issued by one company but payment is requested to another
  • A mainland supplier asks for payment to an unexplained overseas entity

There can be legitimate reasons for a Hong Kong trading entity, group company, or export agent to receive payment. The relationship should be documented and independently confirmed.

5. Verify manufacturing capability separately

A business licence does not prove that a company owns a factory. To assess actual capability, request evidence such as:

  • Factory address and production-line photographs
  • Live video walkthrough
  • Machine list and production capacity
  • Quality-control flow
  • Recent test reports or certifications relevant to the product
  • Sample production and pre-production sample approval
  • Factory audit or independent inspection

For higher-value orders, arrange an on-site visit or third-party audit. Confirm whether critical processes are completed in-house or subcontracted.

6. Check commercial consistency

Reliable suppliers usually provide consistent answers across sales, engineering, quality, and finance teams. Compare:

  • Quoted lead time versus stated production capacity
  • MOQ versus the factory’s normal production method
  • Product claims versus available test evidence
  • Sample quality versus production specifications
  • Payment terms versus the supplier’s risk profile

Very low pricing, unusually fast delivery, and broad claims across unrelated product categories should trigger deeper checks.

7. Use a layered verification decision

8. Red flags that justify pausing payment

  • Refusal to provide the Chinese legal company name
  • Bank details changed through email only
  • Pressure to pay immediately to preserve a “special price”
  • Contradictory factory addresses
  • Edited or unreadable licence documents
  • Samples that do not match the quotation specification
  • Claims of major certifications without verifiable documents
  • No willingness to permit inspection for a meaningful order

A practical verification workflow

  1. Collect the supplier’s Chinese legal information.
  2. Check the official registration record.
  3. Confirm the contact through an independent company channel.
  4. Compare contract, invoice, and beneficiary details.
  5. Approve a written product specification.
  6. Review samples and production capability.
  7. Use a pilot order, inspection, or audit according to risk.
  8. Release payment only after inconsistencies are resolved.

Bottom line

Supplier verification is not a single database search. It is a chain of matching evidence. The stronger the order value, customization, compliance exposure, or payment risk, the deeper the checks should be.
